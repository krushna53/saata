The Cave and Light

I did my TA101 in 2015 and have been in training and supervision since. I passed my CTA in 2023. Eight years! Training and certification in TA are a long process and I have often wondered about this. Recently I read about the Allegory of the Cave - a philosophical concept written by Plato in his book - The Republic. It describes a group of people chained in a cave, facing a blank wall with a fire burning outside the cave behind them. People outside the cave, carrying various objects would pass in front of this fire, projecting shadows onto a wall that the prisoners see. All their life, these shadows are the only reality the prisoners have, and they give names to these shadows.

Imprisonment in the cave

The prisoners, confined to this cave, can only witness the shadows projected on the wall before them. As the shadows move, the sound of people talking reverberates, and the prisoners assume that the sounds are coming from the shadows. They are unaware that the shadows are mere imitations of objects situated in front of the fire. TA for me has been that journey that allowed me to see beyond my shadows. To recognize and acknowledge the cave, the shadows, the possibility of looking at things differently takes time and courage. I believe, being invited to this realization has been the most significant in my TA training.

Departure from the cave

The allegory then talks about how when a prisoner is released out of the cave, he would look around and see the fire. For eyes that have looked only at shadows so far, this is quite a painful experience. The light hurt his eyes and upturns his idea of reality. This liminal experience, the disorientation and discomfort, makes it difficult to acknowledge what he sees as real. This is a difficult time in the learning journey when one sees unhealthy patterns become so clear and yet, moving ahead feels like an impossible task. At this point, transformation and growth can seem improbable. I see this as a significant phase in the life of a practitioner. This immersive experience of personal change helps remain present to the liminal experience of the client as well.

Return to the cave

Soon the freed prisoner, starts to see the world around him in its full glory. Having experienced this superior way of living, he returns to the cave attempting to take the others on the journey he has just experienced. He takes on a one up position because of course he has seen more and pities his fellow cave dwellers and their experience. But when he comes back, he cannot see in the darkness of the cave because his eyes have become accustomed to light. His fellow cave dwellers see this and begin to think that his journey outside has harmed him and that going outside is risky. They reject the one who claims to have seen the light. Having caught glimpses of light, it is easily possible to discount another’s experience. In one’s training and certification process, one is repeatedly invited to live the philosophical underpinnings of TA and I see huge value in it. I believe that my TA training so far has been intense journey of change, challenging my scripty beliefs. I have grown, changed, sometimes gone back to my scripty ways of being, caught myself as I fell and then got up again. This, I believe has allowed me to clearly see my role as a practitioner as I travel along with my clients. It has not just been just about my personal growth; it has readied me to use myself in the service of the greater good. It also has allowed me to acknowledge that learning is a continuous process and that liminal experiences can be uncomfortable and yet, to trust the light. The CTA exam has been an opportunity for me to reflect on my journey, take on my identity as a TA practitioner with power and grace.


- Rosemary Kurian
